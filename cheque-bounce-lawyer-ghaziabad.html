Quick Legal Snapshot: Section 138 NI Act

  • Maximum Punishment

    Under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, the penalty is up to 2 Years Imprisonment and/or a fine up to twice the cheque amount.

  • Statutory Notice Period

    A legal demand notice must be sent within 30 days of the cheque bounce, giving the drawer exactly 15 days to pay.

  • Filing Deadline

    The criminal complaint must be filed in the Chief Judicial Magistrate (CJM) Court, Ghaziabad/Ghaziabad within 30 days after the 15-day notice period expires.

  • Key Defense Strategy

    If you are accused, immediate priority is securing Regular Bail and filing for a Non-Bailable Warrant (NBW) Stay.

How to Defend a False Cheque Bounce Case?

Being falsely accused under Section 138 is stressful, but there are strong statutory defenses available. We secure immediate Bail and construct a defense based on:

No Legally Enforceable Debt

Proving the cheque was not given for discharging a debt (e.g., given as a gift, donation, or advance for unfulfilled work).

Misuse of Security Cheque

Establishing that a blank cheque given merely as security was intentionally misused by filling an arbitrary amount.

Material Alteration

Under Section 87 of the NI Act, any unauthorized alteration (changing the date or amount) renders the cheque void.

Time-Barred Debt

A cheque issued for a debt that is already time-barred (older than 3 years) cannot attract Section 138.

CRITICAL ERRORS

What Mistakes Ruin a Cheque Bounce Case?

01
Delayed Notice serving

Serving notice after the 30-day statutory limit makes the criminal case non-maintainable.

02
Notice Content Errors

Inaccurate mention of cheque number, amount, or date in the legal demand notice.

03
Ignoring Bank Memo

Failing to file the original bank return memo with the court complaint.

04
Incorrect Jurisdiction

Filing the case in a court where no cause of action (bounce/notice) occurred.

05
Defense Loophole

Failing to prove the existence of a "Legally Enforceable Debt" in the complaint.

06
Premature Filing

Filing before the 15-day notice period for payment has actually expired.

Landmark Supreme Court Judgments on Cheque Bounce

Dashrath Rupsingh Rathod v. State of Maharashtra

Key Principle: Clarified the jurisdiction for filing a complaint. Now, cases can primarily be filed where the payee's bank branch is located, streamlining the process for the complainant.

Kisanrao vs. State of Maharashtra (2018)

Key Principle: Reaffirmed the Presumption in favor of the holder under Section 139. The court presumes the cheque was issued for the discharge of a legally enforceable debt, placing the burden of proof heavily on the accused to rebut it.

Surinder Singh Deswal v. Virender Gandhi (2019)

Key Principle: Solidified Section 143A, making it almost mandatory for the trial court to order the accused to pay 20% of the cheque amount as interim compensation to the complainant during the trial.
